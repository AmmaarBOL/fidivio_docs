# Calculations and Outputs

This chapter explains the underlying calculations for each of the core components. Where relevant, the applicable accounting rules are provided.

---

## 6.1 Deferred Revenue Dashboard

The Deferred Revenue Dashboard is the contract liability ledger — the period-by-period accounting for the deferred revenue balance that is created when loyalty points are issued and released as revenue when they are redeemed or broken.

Every IFRS/GAAP revenue line on the P&L flows from this ledger. These calculations determine exactly when and how much revenue the model recognises.

### 6.1.1 Roll-Forward Structure

The Deferred Revenue Dashboard opens with the prior period closing balance, adds new obligations created this period, deducts the revenue released through redemptions and breakage, applies any estimate adjustment, and produces the new closing balance.

```
  Opening Balance At The Start Of The Period
+ Liability Created
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= Liability Balance Before Revenue Recognition
− Revenue Recognized from Redemptions
− Revenue Recognized from Breakage
± Adjustment to Deferred Revenue
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= Closing Balance At The End Of The Period
```

---

### 6.1.2 Opening Liability

The opening balance is the contract liability carried into the current period from the prior period. In Period 1 it is the legacy deferred revenue inherited from any prior program (zero for a new program). In all subsequent periods it is the closing balance produced by the previous period.

### 6.1.3 Calculating the Opening Liability

```
Period 1:  = Opening balance input
Period t:  = Closing Balance of Period t−1
```

*This linkage — the Closing Balance feeding the Opening Balance of the next period — is what makes the ledger continuous. Every period begins from where the last one ended.*

| Scenario | Value and Behaviour |
|---|---|
| **Period 1** | Zero for a new programme. If an existing programme is being transferred in, this equals the deferred revenue balance carried forward from the prior system. |
| **Periods 2 onwards** | Automatically picks up the prior period closing liability. No manual input is required. |

---

### 6.1.4 New Liability Created

Each period, issuing new points creates a new obligation. The line item 'Liability Created' records this addition to the ledger. It is a direct reference to the gross deferred revenue calculated in the liability creation block (Applied SSP × Points Issued).

When points are issued, a liability is created equal to the value of the future performance obligation. The amount depends on the **Applied SSP** — the standalone selling price per point after reflecting the proportion of points expected never to be redeemed (breakage) (when Parameter 4 is set to Option 1).

### 6.1.5 Calculating the New Liability Created

```
Applied SSP × Points issued
```

Where Applied SSP:
- P4=1: `Applied SSP = SSP_input × (1 − breakage_rate)`
- P4=2: `Applied SSP = SSP_input (face value)`

*Parameter 4 determines whether the SSP is adjusted for breakage. Under P4=1 the user enters the face-value SSP and the model scales it down. Under P4=2, no adjustment is made to the SSP for breakage.*

**Exception for Conventional Programs (Parameter 1 = Option 2)**

Total consideration is first allocated between goods and points using relative SSPs (IFRS 15 proportionate allocation). New contract liability = `(points_SSP / total_SSP) × total_consideration`

<Info>
**Key Point:** The line item 'Liability Created' is the only row that can only increase the liability. Every other movement either reduces it (redemptions, breakage) or adjusts it up or down (Adjustment to Deferred Revenue). A growing liability over time means new obligations are being created faster than existing ones are being discharged.
</Info>

<Note>
**Accounting rule (IFRS 77):** The stand-alone selling price is the price at which an entity would sell a promised good or service separately to a customer.
</Note>

<Note>
**Accounting rule (IFRS B44):** Upon receipt of a prepayment from a customer, an entity shall recognise a contract liability in the amount of the prepayment for its performance obligation to transfer, or to stand ready to transfer, goods or services in the future. An entity shall derecognise that contract liability (and recognise revenue) when it transfers those goods or services and, therefore, satisfies its performance obligation.
</Note>

---

### 6.1.6 Liability Before Revenue Recognition

Before any revenue is released in the current period, the total pool of deferred obligation must be established. The line item **Liability Balance Before Revenue Recognition** is that subtotal — the sum of the opening balance and the new liability just created.

### 6.1.7 Calculating the Liability Before Revenue Recognition

```
= Opening liability + New liability created
```

*This figure serves two purposes: it is a subtotal in the roll-forward and the numerator used to compute revenue per point.*

---

### 6.1.8 Revenue Recognized per Point

Revenue is released from the contract liability at a uniform rate per point. This rate is recalculated every period and applies to both redemption revenue and breakage income.

### 6.1.9 Calculating the Revenue Recognized per Point

```
rev_per_point = Liability_before_recognition ÷ Total_points_in_circulation
```

Where:
- `Liability_before_recognition` = opening_liability + new_liability_this_period
- `Total_points_in_circulation` = opening_points_balance + points_issued_this_period

The denominator differs by Parameter 3:
- **P3=1:** total points balance
- **P3=2:** validity-period adjusted balance
- **P3=3:** closing points balance

The 'Liability before recognition' feeds directly into the revenue recognised per point calculations as the numerator.

<Info>
**When can Rev Per Point change?** Rev per point changes when points issued with a different SSP are added to the balance. P2=2 will also change the SSP. Under P2=3, rev per point will be the same as the applied SSP of the previous period.
</Info>

---

### 6.1.10 Revenue Recognized (Points Redeemed)

When members exercise their rights by redeeming points, the corresponding portion of the contract liability is released as revenue. The line item **Revenue Recognized from Points Redemptions** records this release in the ledger and simultaneously feeds the primary IFRS revenue line on the P&L.

### 6.1.11 Calculating the Revenue Recognized (Points Redeemed)

```
= points_redeemed × rev_per_point
```

Where `rev_per_point = Liability_before_recognition ÷ Total_points_in_circulation`

*The same figure appears twice: as P&L income in the IFRS P&L and as a liability reduction in the roll-forward.*

| Component | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Points redeemed** | Actual redemptions in the period, from the user input. Revenue is only released for points actually redeemed this period. |
| **Rev per point** | Blends the liability value across all outstanding cohorts. Derived each period. Equals the total contract liability balance before recognition divided by the total points in circulation. As redemptions occur and the liability is released, this rate evolves over time. |
| **Total_points_in_circulation** | Opening points balance + points issued this period (under P3=1). Represents the full universe of points against which the liability must be allocated. |

<Note>
**IFRS principle:** Revenue is recognised when the performance obligation is satisfied — when the member redeems. The amount is not simply the SSP of the redeemed point; it is a proportionate share of the total liability at the blended rate, ensuring the entire liability is released exactly as all points exit the system.
</Note>

<Note>
**Accounting rule (IFRS 15, paragraphs 35–37):** For each performance obligation satisfied over time, an entity shall recognise revenue over time by measuring the progress towards complete satisfaction of that performance obligation.
</Note>

---

### 6.1.12 Revenue Recognized (Breakage)

The portion of the liability attributable to points expected never to be redeemed — breakage — is also released progressively as revenue. **Revenue Recognized from Breakage** records this release in the ledger. It is recognised alongside redemption revenue, using the same `rev_per_point` rate, but applied to the volume of points that are 'broken' rather than redeemed.

### 6.1.13 Calculating the Revenue Recognized (Breakage)

```
= breakage_points_recognized × rev_per_point
```

Total breakage points released = breakage_from_projection_or_expiry ± breakage_reconciliation_adjustment (when P3=2)

*Breakage income uses the same Rev_per_point rate as redemption income. Both release the liability at the same per-point rate.*

---

### 6.1.14 Calculating Points Considered as Breakage

The volume of breakage points recognized per period depends on Parameter 3.

#### Parameter 3 = 1: Proportional to Redemptions (Default)

```
breakage_pts = points_redeemed × (Total_points_balance − Points_expected_to_be_redeemed)
               ÷ Points_expected_to_be_redeemed
```

Where:
- `Total_points_balance` = opening + issuance (same period)
- `Points_expected_to_be_redeemed` = cumulative points issued − blended breakage rate − cumulative points redeemed (± breakage_adjustment input)

<Note>
**Accounting rule (IFRS B64):** If an entity expects to be entitled to a breakage amount in a contract liability, the entity shall recognise the expected breakage amount as revenue in proportion to the pattern of rights exercised by the customer.
</Note>

#### Parameter 3 = 2: Proportional to Validity Period

```
breakage_pts = (points_issued_in_cohort × breakage_rate) ÷ validity_months
```

*(summed across all active cohorts in the period)*

*Spreads each cohort's expected breakage evenly over its validity window. Requires a validity period input (months).*

#### Parameter 3 = 3: Actual Expiry

```
Breakage_pts = Points_expired (actual)
```

*No forward estimate. Breakage income only arises when points are formally removed from member accounts. Periods with no expiry events show zero breakage income.*

<Note>
**Accounting rule (IFRS B46):** If an entity does not expect to be entitled to a breakage amount, the entity shall recognise the expected breakage amount as revenue when the likelihood of the customer exercising its remaining rights becomes remote.
</Note>

---

### 6.1.15 Breakage Reconciliation Adjustment (P3=2)

An additional reconciliation adjustment is available to correct for any difference between predicted and actual expiry outcomes. It is the `breakage_reconciliation` input, entered in points, and is added to the projection-based breakage volume before converting to income:

```
breakage_pts (total) = breakage_from_projection + breakage_reconciliation_input
```

*Note: if the reconciliation causes the point balance to go negative, the model caps the reconciliation at the available balance.*

*Positive reconciliation = more breakage than predicted (additional release). Negative = less breakage than predicted (clawback).*

---

### 6.1.16 Adjustment to Deferred Revenue

When estimates change — whether the breakage rate, the SSP, or a manual override — the liability balance may need to be restated. The line item **Adjustment to Deferred Revenue** routes the active adjustment into the roll-forward. It is governed by **Parameter 2** (Deferred Revenue True-up).

Whether this line is populated depends on Parameter 2:

#### P2 = 1 — No Automatic True-up (Default)

No catch-up is calculated. The liability balance is not restated for changes in breakage or SSP. A **manual adjustment** field is available for discretionary entries. Estimate changes flow through gradually via the changing `rev_per_point` rate in future periods rather than as a one-period catch-up.

```
= manual_adjustment_input
```

*Set to zero unless the operator makes an explicit manual entry.*

#### P2 = 2 — True-up for Breakage Rate Change

When the breakage rate for any prior issuance period changes, the model computes the difference between the liability that should have been created under the new rate and the liability actually created. This difference is recognised as a catch-up in the current period:

```
true_up = breakage_adjustment_pts × Applied_SSP
```

Where:
- `breakage_adjustment_pts` = adjustment_to_breakage input (points)
- Positive input (more breakage expected): liability decreases → positive P&L

*Only applicable when SSP is adjusted for breakage (P4 = 1). Under P4=2, a change in breakage rate does not affect the contract liability level since the full face-value SSP is used regardless.*

#### P2 = 3 — True-up for SSP Change

When the Applied SSP changes, all outstanding points are revalued to the new SSP. The entire closing balance of points — regardless of issuance period — is remeasured:

```
true_up = (outstanding_points × new_Applied_SSP) − current_liability_balance
```

Where:
- `outstanding_points` = total points balance (closing, per the points ledger)
- Higher SSP: outstanding obligation increases → liability tops up → negative P&L
- Lower SSP: outstanding obligation decreases → liability releases → positive P&L

*This can create significant P&L volatility in periods of SSP revision, as the full stock of outstanding points is revalued simultaneously.*

<Note>
**Sign convention:** A positive true-up value in the P&L represents a release of liability (revenue). A negative value represents a top-up of liability (charge). In the model, the adjustment is expressed as a debit/credit to the liability account, with the opposite sign flowing to the P&L.
</Note>

---

### 6.1.18 Closing Liability

At the end of the ledger is the **'Closing Balance At The End Of The Period'** — the closing balance of the contract liability for the period. It brings all movements together and carries forward as the next period opening balance.

### 6.1.19 Calculating the Closing Liability

```
Closing_liability = Opening_liability
                  + New_liability_created
                  − Revenue_recognized (redemptions)
                  − Breakage_income
                  ± True-up_adjustment (if P2 = 2 or 3)
```

*The closing balance of one period becomes the opening balance of the next. Over the programme lifetime, the balance trends to zero as all points are redeemed, expire, or break.*

| Behaviour | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Growing balance** | New obligations exceed releases. Normal in an expanding programme. |
| **Stable balance** | Issuances, redemptions, and breakage reach equilibrium. The closing balance is roughly constant from period to period. |
| **Declining to zero** | Over the full programme life all points exit the system. Cumulative releases equal cumulative additions and the balance reaches zero. |

---

## 6.2 Relationship Between Deferred Revenue and IFRS P&L

The IFRS P&L draws directly from the Deferred Revenue dashboard for three specific line items:

| IFRS P&L Line | Source |
|---|---|
| Revenue recognized (redemptions) | `Points_redeemed × Rev_per_point` |
| Breakage income | `Breakage_pts × Rev_per_point` |
| Adjustment for liability true-up | `= −Adjustment to Deferred Revenue` (sign flipped: a positive Adjustment to Deferred Revenue = a negative P&L charge) |

## 6.3 IFRS / GAAP P&L

The IFRS P&L applies IFRS 15 revenue recognition. Consideration received for points is deferred at issuance and recognised progressively as performance obligations are satisfied.

### 6.3.1 Structure of the IFRS P&L

```
  Revenue Recognised (Driven by Redemptions)
+ Marketing Income
+ Breakage Income
+ Non-Point Revenue
± Adjustment for Liability True-up (If Any)
− Total Cost of Redemptions
− Program Running Cost
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= IFRS Profit
= Profit Margin %
```

<Info>
**Basis:** Revenue is recognised when — and in proportion to — performance obligations being discharged, not when cash is received. Points issued create a deferred liability (contract liability) that is released as points are redeemed or broken.
</Info>

### 6.3.2 Foundation: The Contract Liability

All IFRS revenue lines flow from a single ledger: the contract liability (also called deferred revenue). See Section 6.1 (Deferred Revenue Dashboard) for detailed calculations on Revenue recognized from redemptions, revenue recognized from breakage, and Adjustment to Deferred Revenue.

### 6.3.3 Line Item: Marketing Income

Marketing income represents the portion of consideration received for points that can be recognised **immediately at issuance**, because it is not subject to any future performance obligation. It arises when the price charged per point (yield) exceeds the Applied SSP.

### 6.3.4 Calculating Marketing Income

```
= (yield_per_point − Applied_SSP) × points_issued
```

Where Applied_SSP depends on P4 setting:
- P4 = 1: `Applied_SSP = SSP_input × (1 − breakage_rate)`
- P4 = 2: `Applied_SSP = SSP_input`

*Only the excess of yield over SSP is recognised immediately. The SSP portion is always deferred as a contract liability. Marketing income is zero when yield = SSP, which is common in self-funded programmes.*

<Info>
**Typical outcome:** In many programme structures, yield is set equal to or close to the Applied SSP, resulting in zero or minimal marketing income. Marketing income is most significant in third-party profit-centre programmes where the loyalty operator charges a premium above the redemption value.
</Info>

### 6.3.5 Line Item: Non-Points Revenues

Non-points revenues are all income earned by the loyalty programme that does not arise from the sale of points. Examples include brand licensing fees, access to member data, technology services provided to partners, and co-marketing arrangements.

### 6.3.6 Calculating Non-Points Revenues

```
= sum of the non_points_revenue (direct input per period)
```

*No deferral. Revenue is recognised in full in the period it is received. This reflects that the associated performance obligations (e.g. provision of data access) are fully satisfied at the point of sale.*

<Note>
This line is identical in both the IFRS P&L and the Adjusted EBITDA P&L. It is unaffected by any of the three accountancy configuration settings.
</Note>

### 6.3.7 Total Cost of Redemptions

The actual cost incurred by the programme to fulfil points redeemed in the period. This is a cash cost, not deferred.

```
= points_redeemed × cost_per_point_redeemed
```

*Both inputs are per-period values direct inputs from the user. `Cost_per_point_redeemed` is the average blended cost across all redemption types in that period.*

### 6.3.8 Program Running Costs

All operational overhead of running the loyalty programme, excluding the direct cost of fulfilling redemptions. Examples include staffing, technology platforms, office costs, and management fees.

```
= program running costs
```

*Direct input from the user. This line is identical in both the IFRS P&L and the Adjusted EBITDA P&L. It is unaffected by any of the three accountancy configuration settings.*

### 6.3.9 Calculating the IFRS Profit

```
= Total_Revenues − Total_Costs

= (Revenue_redemptions + Breakage_income + Marketing_income
 + Non-points_revenues + True-up_adjustment)
− (Cost_of_redemptions + Program_running_costs)
```

## 6.4 Adjusted EBITDA P&L

### 6.4.1 Statement Structure

The Adjusted EBITDA P&L presents programme economics on a **gross billings basis**: revenue is recognised when points are issued, not when they are redeemed. Three potential adjustments then correct for differences between expected and actual programme behaviour, producing a measure of sustainable economic performance.

```
  Gross Billings
+ Non-Point Revenue
− Total Cost of Redemptions
− Program Running Cost
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= EBITDA

± Adjustment for Future Redemption
± Adjustment for Change in Cost Per Point
± Adjustment for Change in Breakage
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= Adjusted EBITDA
```

<Info>
**Basis:** Unlike IFRS, revenue is not deferred. The program recognises the full proceeds from point issuances in the period they occur. The three adjustments then smooth for volatility caused by timing mismatches, cost changes, and estimate revisions.
</Info>

### 6.4.2 Line Item: Gross Billings from Points

Gross Billings represents the total proceeds received from the issuance of loyalty points in the period. It is the top-line revenue measure under the Adjusted EBITDA view.

```
= points_issued × yield_per_point
```

*Both inputs are direct per-period values. This equals the cash received (or receivable) from partners for points sold in the period.*

<Note>
The two sub-lines sum exactly to Gross Billings from Points. They are analytical only and do not affect the total. The split uses the per-period breakage rate input applied to that period's issuances.
</Note>

### 6.4.3 Calculating the Gross Profit (EBITDA)

```
= Total_Gross_Billings − Total_Costs

= (Gross_Billings_from_Points + Non-points_revenues)
− (Cost_of_redemptions + Program_running_costs)
```

*Gross Profit before adjustments. Can be volatile if redemption volumes, issuance volumes, or costs fluctuate materially from period to period.*

---

### 6.4.4 The Potential Adjustments

Gross Profit under the Adjusted EBITDA view captures revenue at issuance but costs at redemption. This timing mismatch — and changes in the estimates underpinning the programme — can cause Gross Profit to be a misleading measure of economic performance in any given period. The three adjustments correct for this:

| Adjustment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Adjustment 1** | Corrects for the difference between actual and expected redemption volumes in the period. |
| **Adjustment 2** | Corrects for changes in the cost per point redeemed versus prior periods. |
| **Adjustment 3** | Corrects for changes in the breakage outlook. |

---

### 6.4.5 Adjustment 1 — Redemption Pattern

Gross Billings from Points is recognised when points are issued, based on the expected redemption rate. Redemption costs, however, are recognised when actual redemptions occur. When actual redemptions in a period differ from expectations, an imbalance arises between the revenue (already recognised at issuance) and the costs (recognised now). Adjustment 1 corrects this.

```
= (Expected points-redeemed − actual points redeemed in current period) × latest_CPP
```

Where:
- `expected_points_redeemed = points_issued × (1 − breakage_rate)`
- `latest_CPP = cost_per_point_redeemed (current period)`

| Scenario | Effect and Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **actual < expected (shortfall)** | Adjustment is negative. Revenue has been recognised for more redemptions than actually occurred. The model provisions for the cost of future redemptions that have not yet happened. A shortfall means the programme has collected money for obligations not yet discharged. |
| **actual > expected (excess)** | Adjustment is positive. Actual redemptions exceeded prediction, meaning redemption costs were higher than anticipated. The excess adjusts back toward the level implied by the original billings. Prior-period provisions are partially released. |
| **actual = expected** | Adjustment is zero. Revenue recognised at issuance perfectly matches the pattern of actual cost discharge. No correction needed. |

---

### 6.4.6 Adjustment 2 — Change in Cost Per Point

The cost per point redeemed (CPP) can change over time as redemption mix, reward pricing, or procurement costs shift. When CPP changes, the provision for the cost of all outstanding, unredeemed points changes accordingly. Adjustment 2 restates this provision to reflect the current CPP.

```
Step 1: Calculate CPP change
  diff_CPP = latest_CPP − previous_period_CPP

Step 2: Apply to future outstanding points
  adj_2 = diff_CPP × points_still_expected_to_be_redeemed_in_future
```

Where:
- `points_still_expected_to_redeem = cumulative_expected_to_redeem − cumulative_actually_redeemed`
- If `diff_CPP = 0`: `adj_2 = 0`

| Scenario | Effect |
|---|---|
| **CPP increases** | diff_CPP is positive. Future redemptions will cost more. Adjustment is negative (additional provision). |
| **CPP decreases** | diff_CPP is negative. Future redemptions will cost less. Adjustment is positive (release of over-provision). |
| **CPP unchanged** | Adjustment is zero. |

<Note>
**Scope:** The adjustment applies only to points still outstanding and expected to be redeemed in the future. Points already redeemed have already been costed at the actual CPP incurred, so no restatement is needed for past activity.
</Note>

---

### 6.4.7 Adjustment 3 — Change in Breakage Estimate

When the programme's view of ultimate breakage changes — i.e. the proportion of points expected never to be redeemed shifts — the expected volume of future redemptions also changes, which in turn changes the expected future cost burden. Adjustment 3 corrects for this.

```
adj_3 = − breakage_adjustment_pts × latest_CPP
```

Where:
- `breakage_adjustment_pts` = adjustment_to_breakage input (points)
- Positive input (more breakage, fewer redemptions expected): → adj_3 is positive (future cost provision released)
- Negative input (less breakage, more redemptions expected): → adj_3 is negative (additional future cost provisioned)

*It represents a retrospective revision to the expected number of outstanding points that will break. It is expressed in points, not as a rate.*

<Info>
**Relationship to IFRS:** Under IFRS User Option 2=2, the same breakage revision also triggers a true-up to the contract liability. In Adjusted EBITDA, Adjustment 3 performs the analogous function but on a cash-cost basis (using CPP) rather than an SSP-deferred-revenue basis.
</Info>

<Info>
**Interpretation:** In steady state (redemption rate matches the breakage rate assumption), Adjustment 1 will be small and stable. It becomes material when the program burn/earn rate is much lower than predicted breakage.
</Info>

---

### 6.4.8 Calculating Adjusted EBITDA

```
= Gross_Profit
± Adjustment_1 (redemption pattern)
± Adjustment_2 (change in cost per point)
± Adjustment_3 (change in breakage estimate)
```

*In steady state with stable estimates, all three adjustments approach zero and Adjusted EBITDA converges to Gross Profit. Adjustments only become material when actual behaviour departs from predictions.*

<Info>
**Design intent:** Adjusted EBITDA is designed to approximate the sustainable economic earnings of the programme in each period — stripped of IFRS timing effects (deferral and release of deferred revenue) and corrected for the variance between expected and actual programme experience. It is intended to be stable and predictable in mature, well-calibrated programmes.
</Info>

## 6.5 Relationship Between the Two P&Ls

### 6.5.1 Conceptual Differences

| Dimension | IFRS vs Adjusted EBITDA |
|---|---|
| **Revenue timing** | IFRS: deferred at issuance; recognised as obligations are discharged. Adjusted EBITDA: recognised at issuance (gross billings basis). |
| **Breakage** | IFRS: recognised proportionally over time as part of the liability release rate (depending on P3 setting). Adjusted EBITDA: embedded in the Gross Billings split; corrected via Adjustment 3 if estimates change. |
| **Estimate changes** | IFRS: absorbed via the changing rev_per_point rate (P2=1) or via the explicit true-up line (P2=2 or 3). Adjusted EBITDA: corrected via Adjustments 1, 2, and 3. |
| **Liability** | IFRS: a contract liability (deferred revenue) is maintained on the balance sheet. Its change each period explains the gap between IFRS and Adjusted EBITDA profit. Adjusted EBITDA: no deferred revenue concept. Revenue is fully recognised at issuance. |

---

## 6.6 Reconciliation (Bridge) Between the Two P&Ls

The model provides a formal reconciliation from Adjusted EBITDA to IFRS Profit. This confirms that the two views are arithmetically consistent:

```
IFRS_Profit = Adjusted_EBITDA
            − Change_in_deferred_revenue_balance (closing − opening liability)
            − Adjustment_1 (redemption pattern)
            − Adjustment_2 (change in CPP)
            − Adjustment_3 (change in breakage)
```

*The change in deferred revenue is the key bridging item: when the liability increases (more revenue deferred than released), IFRS profit is lower than Adjusted EBITDA. When the liability decreases (more revenue released than deferred), IFRS profit is higher.*

<Info>
**Bridge to Adjusted EBITDA:** The change in the Liability Balance (closing minus opening liability) is the key reconciling item between Adjusted EBITDA and IFRS Profit. When the liability grows, IFRS profit is lower than Adjusted EBITDA by exactly that amount. When it shrinks, IFRS profit is higher. Over the programme lifetime the two views converge to the same cumulative total.
</Info>

### 6.6.1 Long-Run Convergence

Over the full lifetime of the programme — when all points have been redeemed, expired, or broken — the cumulative IFRS Profit and cumulative Adjusted EBITDA must be equal. The timing differences between the two views net to zero over the programme's life.
